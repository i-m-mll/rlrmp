This is a fascinating puzzle, and your project is probing a fundamental distinction between classical optimal control formulations and the functional solutions discovered by recurrent neural networks. 

Here is my independent analysis of your framing, the structural flaw in your MVP, and a concrete plan to definitively answer the decoupling question.

### 1. Is the framing right?
**Yes, the framing is fundamentally correct.** 

$\Delta v$ (peak velocity inflation) is an elegant and correct behavioral signature for distinguishing a pure regulator from a decoupled controller. 

In a pure regulator $u_t = -K_t (x_t - x_{target})$, the nominal driving force is entirely generated by the feedback gain matrix $K_t$ acting on the error relative to the target. If adversarial training demands higher robustness (disturbance rejection), the optimizer **must** increase $K_t$. Because $K_t$ is the only driving engine, scaling it up inevitably drives the system to the target faster. Therefore, $\Delta v > 0$ is a mathematically unavoidable consequence of increasing robustness in a pure regulator.

In a true tracker, nominal drive and feedback correction are split. If the controller can decouple these, increasing robustness only steepens the feedback gradients around the nominal trajectory, without altering the nominal drive itself. Therefore, $\Delta v \approx 0$ (or $\Delta v \le 0$, discussed in Section 4) is the correct signature of a controller that has achieved decoupling.

### 2. Is the linear-architecture MVP approach valid?
**The *concept* is valid, but the *implementation* (`410d7ac`) is structurally degenerate.** Your realization about the trivial $x_{nom}$ is spot on.

In `src/rlrmp/networks/linear_controllers.py`, your `LinearTrackerController` does this:
```python
e = (pos - target_pos, vel)
u = u_ff(t) - K_t * e
```
Because $e_t$ is computed relative to the **static target**, $e_t$ is massive at $t=0$. This forces the feedback term $-K_t e_t$ to act as the primary engine of movement. Even with a free $u_{ff}(t)$ parameter, the dynamics are dominated by the regulator term. When adversarial training inflates $K_t$ for robustness, the massive $-K_t e_t$ term inflates the nominal drive. 

A **minimum viable tracker** MUST parameterize the nominal trajectory $x_{nom}(t)$ and compute error relative to it:
$$e_t = x_t - x_{nom}(t)$$
$$u_t = u_{ff}(t) - K_t e_t$$
For this to decouple, $e_t \approx 0$ during an undisturbed reach. The nominal movement is driven entirely by $u_{ff}(t)$, and $K_t$ only activates when $w_t \neq 0$. 

### 3. What is the best path forward?
To definitively prove or rule out the decoupling hypothesis, I recommend a 3-phase experimental plan.

**Phase A: The Structural Proof of Existence (Fixed Tracker)**
Before trusting the optimizer to *discover* decoupling, prove that the architecture *allows* it.
1. Train a baseline `LinearController` (regulator). Extract its nominal trajectory $\bar{x}(t)$ and control sequence $\bar{u}(t)$.
2. Implement a `StrictLinearTracker` where $x_{nom}(t)$ and $u_{ff}(t)$ are **frozen** to $\bar{x}(t)$ and $\bar{u}(t)$. The *only* trainable parameter is $K_t$.
3. Train this strictly-parameterized tracker under the adversarial regime.
*Prediction:* It will successfully reject the adversary by inflating $K_t$, but will exhibit $\Delta v = 0$ because the nominal drive is locked. This proves decoupling is mechanically possible for the plant.

**Phase B: End-to-End Discovery (Learnable Tracker)**
Test if the optimizer can find the decoupled solution on its own.
1. Make $x_{nom}(t)$ and $u_{ff}(t)$ trainable LTV sequences (shapes `(T, n_states)` and `(T, n_controls)`).
2. **Crucial:** Initialize them to the baseline $\bar{x}(t)$ and $\bar{u}(t)$ to prevent early-training collapse into the regulator manifold.
3. Train adversarially.
*Prediction:* If the optimizer behaves, it will inflate $K_t$ while leaving $x_{nom}$ and $u_{ff}$ largely alone, preserving $\Delta v \approx 0$. 

**Phase C: GRU Representation Analysis (The Ultimate Test)**
You don't need to guess if the GRU is acting as a tracker; you can measure it directly using your existing `src/rlrmp/analysis/induced_gain.py` machinery.
1. Take a baseline GRU and an adversarially-trained GRU. Extract their nominal trajectories (zero perturbation).
2. Linearize both GRUs locally around their respective nominal trajectories. This yields a time-varying open-loop drive $u_{ff}(t)$ and a time-varying sensory feedback matrix $K_t = \partial u_t / \partial x_t$.
3. Compare them.
*Prediction:* You will find that the adversarial GRU has substantially larger $||K_t||$ matrices (proving robustness via feedback), but its $u_{ff}(t)$ profile remains remarkably similar to the baseline GRU (proving decoupling).

### 4. Alternative hypotheses to consider
Why does the GRU show $\Delta v \approx -20\%$ to $-26\%$ instead of exactly $0\%$? 

1. **Velocity Deflation for Safety:** If the GRU were a perfect decoupled tracker that merely solved the baseline task, $\Delta v$ would be $0$. The fact that it is *negative* means it is intentionally changing its nominal trajectory $x_{nom}(t)$ to be slower. In an adversarial setting with finite control authority (actuator limits) and sensory delays, a slower trajectory is inherently safer—it leaves more actuator headroom to fight perturbations and provides more time to correct late-stage errors. The GRU is not just decoupling; it is optimizing the nominal trajectory itself for worst-case safety.
2. **State Estimation / Internal Model Shift:** The GRU is not just a policy; it maintains a belief state $h_t$ over delayed/noisy sensory data (acting as a Kalman filter). Adversarial training might be lowering the GRU's trust in sensory inputs (altering the implicit observer gain) and forcing a heavier reliance on its internal forward model. Moving slower might be a requirement to keep the internal forward model accurate when sensory updates are deemed unreliable.
3. **Bimodality ($\sim 25\%$ positive inflation):** The bimodal distribution you noted is a huge clue. It implies there are two distinct, valid local minima for nonlinear robust control: 
   - *Strategy A (Decoupled Deflation):* Move slow, decouple feedback, maintain safety ($\Delta v < 0$).
   - *Strategy B (Coupled Regulator):* Power through aggressively, relying on massive gains ($\Delta v > 0$).
   The GRU's nonlinearity gives it the capacity to fall into either basin, whereas the linear regulator is forced into Strategy B.

### 5. Anything missing? Literature and Theory
**Linear Quadratic Tracking (LQT) vs. Regulator Formulation.**
In classical optimal control literature (e.g., Anderson & Moore, *Optimal Control*), the Tracking problem is mathematically distinct from the Regulator problem. 
- The LQT solution inherently yields a two-part control law: a feedforward command $v_t$ (solved backward in time from the reference trajectory) and a feedback gain $K_t$ (the standard Riccati solution operating on $x_t - r_t$). 
- Crevecoeur & Scott (2019) frame the reach strictly as a **Regulator** problem where the origin is the target. They rely entirely on a heavily back-loaded, time-varying cost schedule ($Q_t \propto (t/N)^6$) to force the system to move. 

Because C&S *chose* a regulator formulation, their analytical H-infinity controller is mathematically prohibited from decoupling. The "velocity inflation" signature might partly be an artifact of applying H-infinity to a strict regulator formulation with a polynomial cost schedule, rather than a universal law of optimal robust movement. The GRU, unconstrained by the Riccati formulation and operating on a continuous (or simpler) loss function, naturally discovers the more efficient LQT/decoupled regime. 

**Summary:** Your framing is excellent. Fix the MVP by parameterizing $x_{nom}(t)$, initialize it via a warm-start, and lean into `induced_gain.py` to pull the local $K_t$ directly out of the GRU.
